What success should reveal

A balanced enablement scorecard.

No single adoption count or model metric proves this role is working. The scorecard must expose trust, work, value, control, and learning at the same time.

Trust
Can people find the right data, understand its definition, and identify its owner?
Example signals: ownership coverage, quality exceptions, definition alignment
Use
Are teams using the AI-assisted workflow after the launch moment?
Example signals: retained use, repeat behavior, champion coverage
Flow
Did the intervention reduce delay, handoffs, error, or rework?
Example signals: cycle time, exception age, first-pass quality
Value
Did the workflow produce defensible time, cost, risk, service, or growth benefit?
Example signals: realized benefit, avoided burden, time to value
Control
Are human authority, approved data, review points, and escalation paths explicit?
Example signals: guardrail coverage, exception closure, auditability
Learning
How quickly does observed friction become a revised standard or product decision?
Example signals: feedback-to-change time, reusable patterns, retired work

The strongest reasonable concern

Do not confuse honesty with weakness.

The role mentions SQL, data lakes, and Fabric. The credible case is not to relabel Russell as a career enterprise data architect. It is to show exactly where his operating leadership adds leverage - and where deep platform specialists should be used.

What I will not claim

  • Long tenure under a formal enterprise data architect title
  • Ownership of a production Microsoft Fabric estate not supported by evidence
  • That platform selection alone solves fragmented definitions or adoption

What I will own

  • Business architecture, data meaning, workflow prioritization, decision rights, governance, adoption, and value realization
  • Hands-on SQL, Python, API, ERP, CRM, analytics, and AI workflow work within verified experience
  • Clear vendor and specialist boundaries, with internal ownership that remains after consultants leave
First 120 days

Earn the right to scale.

Start with one decision-critical slice. Make its data, ownership, authority, workflow, and evidence visible. Then compound the pattern rather than launching a broad tool campaign.

Days 1-30

Map reality

  • Inventory systems, critical data, current AI use, vendors, access, and risk
  • Trace high-friction workflows with leaders and users
  • Select one bounded proving workflow
Days 31-60

Establish trust

  • Name owners and canonical definitions
  • Set quality, lineage, access, and exception rules
  • Define platform and vendor responsibilities
Days 61-90

Embed one intervention

  • Define AI role and human authority
  • Insert into real work with training and support
  • Instrument retained use and workflow outcome
Days 91-120

Compound the pattern

  • Standardize reusable definitions, controls, and connectors
  • Establish portfolio and vendor cadence
  • Scale, revise, hold, or retire based on evidence

Questions for discovery

What I would ask next.

Because the end client is confidential, the most important next step is discovery - not pretending public information reveals internal reality.

Which fragmented data domain or workflow is currently causing the most consequential delay, rework, or executive uncertainty?

Is the role expected to personally engineer the platform, own the architecture and vendors, or deliberately combine both?

What has already been attempted with Copilot, Claude, or automation - and where did trust, access, usefulness, or adoption break down?

Which business leader is prepared to own the first workflow outcome rather than delegate AI to technology?

Which regulatory, contractual, privacy, or model-risk boundaries shape the firm's acceptable AI use?

At twelve months, what visible operating behavior would make leadership say this capability has become part of the firm?
